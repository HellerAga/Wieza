Step 2-Analyzing the site by incorporating source material into a lesson plan for an undergraduate course, Family Diversity in America, using Nielsen’s Usability Heuristics. The lesson plan includes original information sources students can retrieve from the Tower digital collection. The sources must consist of the context of diversity in understanding family-related social problems and social policies.


The top 2 weaknesses of the system are as follows:
  1. Flexibility and efficiency of use. The system lacks filters for browsing, sorting, and modifiers, i.e., date range, section, type, and sort by relevance. Results after typing “family diversity” in a search box are disappointing. Many articles and sources are unrelated to the actual search. The articles’ hierarchy is inconsistent, and related stories should be suggested in the navigations. Some articles are long, and separating parts with a relevant image might enhance readability and benefit nero-divergent users.
  2. Consistency and standards. The articles’ hierarchy is inconsistent, and related stories should be suggested in the navigations. Some articles are long, and separating parts with a relevant image might enhance readability and benefit nero-divergent users. Overall, many articles have issues with consistency and standards. Images and photos don’t include captions. This negatively affects students who use screen readers. Some images are oversized and not proportional to the text and, therefore, might be a distracting and disengaging factor in students’ understanding of the text content.

The top 2 strengths of the system are as follows:
  1. Match between the system and the real world. The content is accessible and easy to read because the language style is consistent with the one the students use. Embedding information sources (articles) with interactive links to social media or campus groups allows students to develop critical thinking. A wide range of comments (some might be controversial and not fact-based) from the local community can be used to facilitate discussion about diversity. The following article, with its embedded comments and links, is an example href="https://cuatower.com/2021/02/controversial-activist-abby-johnson-invited-to-virtual-campus-what-it-means-to-be-pro-life-under-debate"
  2. Aesthetics. The Toweraesthetics is a reminiscence of a print newspaper, with its black title letters on a white background. The color scheme, graphics, and font size are consistent throughout the digital collection. Students will likely find the interface visually attractive, allowing them to stay focused and entertained while browsing for information relevant to their class subject. Horizontal, wider-than-usual text columns can be considered an aesthetic advantage by some end-users.

Summary.
A heuristic analysis of the system and mini usability test observations resulted in similar conclusions about the strengths and weaknesses of the system. Efficiency, standards, and consistency were why the end-user was frustrated with the amount of irrelevant data that came up as a search result. The end-user gave up after being discouraged by the number of irrelevant articles displayed and with none within the World War II context. It was only after typing "Second World War" in a search bar that she was able to retrieve needed information ( Anthropology Department Looks for Those Missing in Action, an article from November 18, 2019. In conclusion, the system requires improvement for more efficient information retrieval and browsing. Tools such as "sorting by" options and modifiers, e.g., date range, section, and type, are necessary to make the system user-friendly and efficient. Additionally, images and photos should include captions, as they are to assist users with visual impairments who use tools like screen readers. The end-user appreciated the system's aesthetics, including its overall design, horizontal text, and bold images.
